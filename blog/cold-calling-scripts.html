Cold Calling is Not Dead in Kenya: The 'CEO' Script for 2026

SB

SmartBiz Sales Team

There is a popular myth in Nairobi's tech and business circles: "Nobody answers their phone anymore. Just send a WhatsApp."

This is false. While low-level employees might ignore calls, **decision-makers (CEOs, Directors, Procurement Heads)** still live on their phones. In fact, in 2026, because everyone else is hiding behind emails and DMs, picking up the phone has become a superpower.

However, the old "Telemarketer Style" (reading a robot script) is dead. If you sound like you are selling insurance or credit cards, you will get hung up on.

This guide breaks down the modern, psychological approach to B2B cold calling in Kenya that actually gets meetings.

1. The Gatekeeper: "Madam is in a meeting"

Before you speak to the boss, you have to get past the receptionist or PA. In Kenya, they are trained to block you. If you sound like a salesperson, you lose.

The Mistake: "Hello, can I speak to the manager in charge of procurement?" (Sounds like a sales call).

The Fix: Sound like you already know them. Use confidence and specificity.

You: "Hi [Receptionist Name, get it from Truecaller if possible], is John around?"
Receptionist: "May I ask who is calling?"
You: "It's Kevin from SmartBiz. He's expecting to hear from me regarding the logistics proposal." (Even if he isn't technically expecting it right now, this implies authority).

2. The Opener: Permission-Based Marketing

Once you get the decision-maker on the line, you have 5 seconds. Do not ask "How are you?" (It's fake and wastes time).

In 2026, we use Permission-Based Openers. This lowers their defense mechanism.

"Hi John, this is Kevin calling from SmartBiz. I know I'm catching you out of the blue, but do you have 30 seconds to let me tell you why I called? Then you can decide if we keep talking."

Why this works: You acknowledge the interruption. You give them control ("You can decide"). 90% of Kenyan execs will say, "Okay, go ahead, be quick."

3. The Pitch: The "I Saw This" Framework

Generic pitches fail. You must prove you did your homework. We call this the "Research Hook."

"Great. The reason I'm calling is I was browsing your website this morning, and I noticed you just opened a new branch in Kisumu."

"Usually, when logistics companies expand to Western, they struggle with tracking the riders in real-time. We actually solved this for [Competitor/Similar Company] last month."

"I wanted to see if this is something on your radar right now?"

This proves you are not a robot. You looked at their business.

4. Handling the "Send me an Email" Objection

This is the classic Kenyan brush-off. "Tutumie email, tutaona." (Send us an email, we'll see).

If you just say "Okay" and send the email, you will never hear back. Use this pivot:

Prospect: "Send me an email."
You: "I can certainly do that, John. But to make sure I don't flood your inbox with irrelevant info, could you tell meâ€”is fixing your rider tracking a priority for Q1, or should I reach out in 6 months?"

This forces them to be honest. If they say "Reach out in 6 months," you save time. If they say "It is a priority," you have permission to push for a meeting.

5. The Call-to-Action (CTA)

The goal of a cold call is NOT to sell. It is to sell the meeting.

Don't try to explain your whole product on the phone. Just get 15 minutes on Zoom or a coffee meeting.

"I don't want to take up more of your time now. Do you have your calendar handy? I can show you how the system works in 15 minutes on Tuesday morning."

6. Tools to Help You

  • Truecaller: Essential in Kenya. Know who you are calling before they pick up.
  • LinkedIn: Check their recent posts to find a conversation starter.
  • SmartBiz AI Commander: Our tool can write these scripts for you automatically based on the company's URL.

Conclusion

Cold calling is a numbers game, but it's also a skill. In a market where everyone is hiding behind WhatsApp texts, the person confident enough to pick up the phone stands out. Be polite, be researched, and be brief.
