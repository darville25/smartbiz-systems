Email Marketing in Kenya: Is It Dead or Just Done Wrong?

SB

SmartBiz Marketing Desk

Ask a typical Nairobi business owner about email marketing, and they will likely laugh. "Kenyans don't read emails," they'll say. "Everyone is on WhatsApp."

On the surface, they seem right. WhatsApp penetration in Kenya is over 93%. SMS is ubiquitous. But if email is dead, why do companies like Jumia, Safaricom, and every major bank still send millions of emails a month?

The Truth: Email is not dead in Kenya. It is just **reserved**. We use WhatsApp to chat with friends and buy vegetables. We use Email to make serious business decisions, sign contracts, and learn professional skills.

If you are selling B2B (Business to Business) services, High-End Real Estate, or Professional Consulting, ignoring email marketing is leaving a massive ROI on the table.

1. The "Database" Myth (Stop Buying Lists!)

Let's address the elephant in the room. The reason most Kenyans hate email marketing is because of spam.

The Wrong Way: Buying a "Database of 10,000 Nairobi CEOs" from a guy in CBD for KES 2,000 and blasting them with a generic PDF flyer.

This does not work. It gets you marked as spam, it ruins your brand reputation, and it is actually illegal under the Data Protection Act of 2019. If you did not get consent, do not send the email.

The Right Way: Inbound Lead Generation. Offer something of value (a Lead Magnet) in exchange for their email. A person who asks to hear from you is 10x more likely to buy than a stranger you spammed.

2. WhatsApp vs. Email: The Battle for Attention

Should you use WhatsApp or Email? The answer is "Both," but for different things.

WhatsApp is for:

  • Transactional updates ("Your rider is here").
  • Quick questions ("Do you have size 42?").
  • Closing a sale that is already hot.

Email is for:

  • Nurturing: Educating a client over weeks so they trust you enough to pay KES 100,000+.
  • Newsletters: Sharing industry insights that position you as an expert.
  • Automation: Sending a sequence of 5 emails to onboard a new client without you lifting a finger.

3. Tools That Work in Kenya

Don't just use Gmail to send BCC emails to 50 people. It looks unprofessional and lands in spam. You need a proper ESP (Email Service Provider).

Mailchimp

The global standard. It's easy to use, has beautiful templates, and offers a free plan up to 500 contacts. However, as your list grows, it gets expensive quickly (charged in USD).

Brevo (formerly Sendinblue)

A favorite for Kenyan SMEs. Why? They charge by emails sent, not by number of contacts. If you have a large list but only send once a month, this is cheaper. They also integrate well with SMS.

Zoho Campaigns

If you are already using Zoho CRM, this is a no-brainer. It syncs perfectly, so you know exactly which lead opened your email and when to call them.

4. How to Write Emails Kenyans Will Read

The "Corporate Speak" of 2010 is dead. "Dear Sir/Madam, kindly find attached..." is boring.

To win in 2025, your emails need to be:

  • Personal: Use their first name. Write like you are talking to one person, not a crowd.
  • Mobile Optimized: 80% of emails in Kenya are opened on a phone. Keep paragraphs short. Use big buttons.
  • Value First: Don't just say "Buy my stuff." Give them a tip, a story, or a market insight. Earn the right to sell.

5. The Legal Side (ODPC Compliance)

The Office of the Data Protection Commissioner (ODPC) is active. To stay safe:

  • Always have an "Unsubscribe" link at the bottom. It's mandatory.
  • Use "Double Opt-In" (they have to confirm their email) to ensure they actually want to be on your list.
  • State clearly in your Privacy Policy how you use their data.

Conclusion

Email marketing in Kenya isn't about blasting thousands of strangers. It's about building a private, owned channel with your best customers. Social media algorithms change; you own your email list forever.

Frequently Asked Questions

Q: What is a good Open Rate in Kenya?

For B2B, anything above 20% is good. For B2C/Retail, 15-18% is standard.

Q: How often should I email?

Consistency matters more than frequency. Once a week or once every two weeks is ideal. Daily is too much for the Kenyan market.
